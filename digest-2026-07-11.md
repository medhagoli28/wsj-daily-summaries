**4. China Successfully Launches Reusable Rocket in a Win for Space Program** — China recovered the first stage of its Long March-10B carrier rocket during an orbital launch from the Wenchang commercial spaceport in Hainan on July 10, 2026, a breakthrough in its race to match SpaceX on reusable technology. About six minutes after stage separation, the roughly 760-ton, five-meter-diameter first stage returned vertically and was captured in a net strung across a sea-based recovery platform — the world's first net-capture recovery of an orbital-class booster and China's first maritime booster recovery. The feat makes China only the second nation ever to recover an orbital-class booster, and officials framed it as filling a domestic gap that will sharply cut commercial launch costs. China plans to refly the very same first stage before the end of the year. *Sources: [CGTN](https://news.cgtn.com/news/2026-07-10/China-achieves-reusable-rocket-breakthrough-with-Long-March-10B-1OF9e3z0u2Y/p.html), [Space.com](https://www.space.com/space-exploration/launches-spacecraft/making-history-china-lands-rocket-during-an-orbital-launch-for-1st-time-ever), [CNN](https://edition.cnn.com/2026/07/10/china/china-reusable-rockets-test-breakthrough-intl-hnk).*

**5. Meta Failed to Protect Users From Addictive Apps, EU Says** — The European Commission issued a preliminary finding on July 10, 2026, that Meta's Facebook and Instagram breach the Digital Services Act through "addictive" design choices, singling out infinite scroll, autoplay, push notifications, and engagement-maximizing recommender systems. **5. The Mind Game That Investors Can't Stop Playing** — The market-timing impulse — trying to sell before drops and buy back before rebounds — is one of the most persistent and costly behavioral traps in investing. It is rooted in loss aversion, the tendency identified by psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky in which the pain of a loss is roughly twice as powerful as the pleasure of an equivalent gain, pushing investors to panic-sell in downturns and chase rallies at the top. Research suggests emotional decision-making shaves 1–2 percentage points off returns annually, and studies of the "behavior gap" find the average investor earns only about 3–4% versus roughly 10% for the market itself. Because the market's best days often cluster right after its worst, investors who flee downturns tend to miss the recovery, locking in losses that compound over time. *Sources: [Morgan Stanley](https://www.morganstanley.com/articles/behavioral-finance), [Allworth Financial](https://allworthfinancial.com/articles/the-myth-of-market-timing), [Berger Financial Group](https://www.bergerfinancialgroup.com/why-market-timing-fails-the-benefits-of-staying-invested-long-term/).*

## 💰 Personal Finance

**1. A Couple Living on One Income Wants to Buy a Home. Can They Afford It?** — Buying a home on a single income turns on the classic affordability math: the 28/36 rule, which says housing costs should stay under 28% of gross monthly income and total debt payments under 36%. Lenders in 2026 are more flexible in practice — most conventional loans allow debt-to-income ratios up to 50% and FHA loans up to about 56.9% — but stretching to those limits leaves little cushion. The backdrop is tough for one-earner households: the median new home runs roughly $413,500, 30-year fixed rates hover around 6.1%, and the median U.S. household income is about $83,150. Advisers stress that a single-income family carrying a car payment or high state taxes can feel "underwater" at the same 30% housing ratio that a dual-income household absorbs comfortably, so building an emergency fund and minimizing other debt matters more than hitting a rule of thumb. *Sources: [Hudson Valley Credit Union](https://www.hvcu.org/learning-center/beyond-the-28-rule/), [AmeriSave](https://www.amerisave.com/learn/how-much-income-do-you-need-to-buy-a-house-in-a-complete-affordability-guide), [Zillow](https://www.zillow.com/mortgage-calculator/house-affordability/).*

**3. Why White-Collar Men Are Obsessed With the Perfect Lawn** — The manicured American lawn is less about grass than about status, control, and belonging — a cultural fixation with deep economic roots. Americans pour an estimated $40–60 billion a year into the turfgrass industry, and lawns and other managed turf cover more than 40 million acres of the continental U.S., an area roughly three times larger than any single irrigated crop. The obsession is historically shallow: imported from European aristocratic estates, it spread widely only after World War II, when federally financed low-cost mortgages fueled tract-housing suburbs whose builders used lawns to signal middle-class arrival. Psychologists note that a lush, tidy yard functions as a public barometer of a household's success and stability, which helps explain why time-strapped professionals treat weekend mowing and edging as a point of pride. *Sources: [Noema Magazine](https://www.noemamag.com/the-cult-of-the-american-lawn/), [Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/blog/anthropology-in-practice/the-american-obsession-with-lawns/), [Freakonomics](https://freakonomics.com/podcast/how-stupid-is-our-obsession-with-lawns/).*
