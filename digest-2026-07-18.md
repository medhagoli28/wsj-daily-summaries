**4. Debt Collectors Chase More Consumers in Court** — Debt-collection lawsuits have surged back to pre-pandemic highs across U.S. state courts as collectors ramp up filings against consumers. **5. The Trigger Clause More Couples Want in Their Prenups** — A growing number of couples are asking to add "infidelity clauses" (also called no-cheating or lifestyle clauses) to prenuptial agreements, imposing a predetermined financial penalty, asset forfeiture, or loss of spousal support if one spouse has an affair. These are one type of broader lifestyle clause that can also cover matters like household responsibilities, weight, in-law visits, and other personal conduct, with noncompliance tied to financial consequences. Enforceability is the central problem: several no-fault divorce states — including California, Nevada, Iowa, and Hawaii — generally refuse to enforce infidelity clauses, and courts are reluctant to police personal behavior within a marriage. Family lawyers note the clauses often function more as a deterrent or a values statement than an enforceable financial term. They also carry interpersonal risk, since merely proposing one can breed distrust before the wedding. *Sources: [FindLaw](https://www.findlaw.com/legalblogs/law-and-life/can-i-include-a-no-cheating-clause-in-my-prenup/), [Bandle & Zaeske](https://www.bandleandzaeske.com/can-a-prenup-or-postnup-with-an-infidelity-clause-deter-spouse-from-cheating/).*

**6. Industry charge-offs of roughly 3.8% remain off the 4.6% cycle peak but slightly above pre-pandemic norms. *Sources: [WalletHub](https://wallethub.com/edu/cc/credit-card-charge-off-delinquency-statistics/25536), [Yahoo Finance](https://finance.yahoo.com/markets/stocks/articles/citigroups-card-delinquencies-decline-charge-150900241.html).*

**7.
