## 💻 Tech

**1. IBM's Big Blue Blues** — IBM shares cratered about 25% on July 14, 2026 — the stock's worst single-day drop on record — after the company issued a preliminary Q2 warning, guiding to roughly $17.2 billion in revenue and $2.93 adjusted EPS versus consensus of about $17.86 billion and $3.02. CEO Arvind Krishna said IBM "did not anticipate the magnitude of the capex reprioritization," as enterprise clients diverted spending in late June toward supply-constrained AI servers, storage, and memory instead of IBM mainframes and software. By segment, infrastructure fell 7%, while software rose 5% and consulting was up just 1%, underscoring how the AI buildout is siphoning dollars away from IBM's legacy franchises. The warning echoed a more than 20% slide in February 2026 driven by fears that advanced AI coding models could automate COBOL modernization and erode IBM's high-margin consulting and mainframe ecosystem. Analysts framed the plunge as a signal of a broader shift in how enterprises allocate IT budgets amid the AI infrastructure crunch. *Sources: [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/14/ibm-warns-second-quarter-earnings-fell-short-of-expectations.html), [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/07/14/ibm-stock-ai-arvind-krishna), [Reuters Breakingviews](https://www.breakingviews.com/columns/breaking-view/ibm-struggles-put-out-ai-fire-2026-07-14/).*

**2. IBM CEO Arvind Krishna Has Nowhere to Hide From AI** — After IBM's roughly 25% crash on July 14, 2026, CEO Arvind Krishna faced pointed scrutiny over whether AI is now hollowing out IBM's core businesses rather than expanding them. Krishna admitted the company "faltered" and "did not adapt and move quickly enough," telling CNBC that clients were pausing new cybersecurity and consulting deals — partly citing pressure from advanced AI models — while reallocating capex toward supply-constrained AI hardware. The preliminary Q2 guide of about $17.2 billion in revenue and $2.93 adjusted EPS missed Wall Street targets, and infrastructure revenue fell 7%. Commentators noted Krishna, who has led IBM since 2020 and bet heavily on hybrid cloud, watsonx AI, and quantum, now has little room to argue IBM is a clear AI winner. *Sources: [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/14/ibm-warns-second-quarter-earnings-fell-short-of-expectations.html), [TheStreet](https://www.thestreet.com/investing/stocks/ibm-ceo-sends-blunt-message-on-ai-and-quantum-computing), [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/07/14/ibm-stock-ai-arvind-krishna).*

## 💰 Personal Finance

**1. This Shortcut to Private-Equity Riches Is Minting Young Millionaires** — The private-equity boom is generating outsized wealth for young professionals primarily through carried interest, the roughly 20% share of fund profits paid to managers after limited partners recover their capital plus a preferred return (commonly an ~8% hurdle). Mega-fund associates already earn $325k-$450k in all-in cash, VPs $450k-$700k, and senior partners $900k-$2M+ before carry, while European lower-tier partners can see carried-interest payouts up to roughly €24m ($29m). A major accelerant is the secondaries and continuation-fund boom: secondary transaction volume topped $225 billion in 2025 (up over 40% year over year), with continuation vehicles making up 89% of GP-led volume and about 43% of the total market, letting GPs crystallize carry early on their best assets. Retail and 401(k) money is now flooding in after President Trump's August 2025 executive order easing access to private equity, real estate and crypto in the $14 trillion defined-contribution market, with Deloitte projecting one in six U.S. retail investor funds will have meaningful private-capital exposure by 2030. Insiders forecast secondaries alone could hit $400 billion by 2030, extending the wealth-creation runway for young PE professionals whose carry can compound into tens of millions over a career. *Sources: [eFinancialCareers](https://www.efinancialcareers.com/news/private-equity-credit-pay), [William Blair](https://www.williamblair.com/-/media/downloads/ib/2026/williamblair-pca-secondary-market-report-survey-2026.pdf), [Deloitte](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/financial-services/financial-services-industry-predictions/2026/private-capital-retail-investor-funds.html).*

**2. A Summer Reading List — The Intelligent Investor** — This evergreen advice column (which shares its name with Benjamin Graham's 1949 classic, The Intelligent Investor, now edited with commentary by columnist Jason Zweig) points readers toward foundational investing books and timeless principles for summer reading. Graham's text, which Warren Buffett called "by far the best book about investing ever written," popularized value investing, the margin of safety, and the parable of "Mr. Market" to teach discipline against emotional decision-making; Buffett credited Graham as a formative influence on his career. Other perennially recommended titles include Burton Malkiel's A Random Walk Down Wall Street, John Bogle's The Little Book of Common Sense Investing (championing low-cost index funds), Charlie Munger's Poor Charlie's Almanack, and Morgan Housel's The Psychology of Money. The common through-lines are evergreen: buy undervalued assets with a margin of safety, keep costs low, diversify, invest for the long term, and control behavioral impulses rather than chasing market timing. These principles endure precisely because they are cheap to follow and largely immune to short-term market noise, making classic investing literature a reliable summer refresher for individual investors. *Sources: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Intelligent_Investor), [Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/106835.The_Intelligent_Investor), [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Intelligent-Investor-Definitive-Investing-Essentials/dp/0060555661).*
